For me education means to build a better model of the world — one that
helps us predict, adapt, and thrive. We've been gathering knowledge for
millennia — if we're serious about what we pass down, we should look
at what we already know and ask what we can do better.

When I read *[The School of Life](/library/books/school-of-life/)* I felt
this is something I actually miss and wanted to learn properly. Attachment
styles, emotional intelligence, how we lead social lives — a big chunk of
life apart from the usual technical education we get. We're expected to
figure this out on our own. The more I read, the more I realized we're failing at this in ways
that are obvious and entirely fixable. Not because the research
doesn't exist. Because nobody acts on it. 

---

### The maturity trap

Gladwell documents something in *Outliers* that I can't stop thinking about. In Canadian hockey, the eligibility cutoff is January 1. In elite groups, 40% of players are born January through March. 10% are born October through December. A twelve-month age gap at five or six is enormous — the older kids aren't necessarily more talented, they're just more mature. They get picked for better teams, get better coaching. Sociologists call it cumulative advantage.

This isn't just sports. It shows up in education. The small initial advantage persists — it locks children into patterns of achievement and underachievement that stretch on for years. Teachers confuse maturity with ability. Researcher Elizabeth Dhuey calls it outlandish that arbitrary cutoff dates cause these long-lasting effects and nobody seems to care.

Denmark's model — no ability grouping until age ten — remains the most cited example of this working. They wait for maturity differences to even out before making any selection decisions. Recent research supports the logic: a 2023 study found the relative age effect holds strong until at least sixteen, which is an argument for tracking students as late as possible, not as early as we do.[^3]

Here's what concerns me beyond the academic side. From what I've learned about psychology and attachment — when a child is young and falling behind, they don't have the tools to understand why. They develop coping mechanisms. They internalize beliefs: *I'm not enough, everyone else is just better.* Those beliefs stretch through an entire life. We're passing on cumulative disadvantage knowingly, out of nothing more than an arbitrary calendar choice.

I believe everyone should be given a fair chance. If we know this effect exists, every state should at least audit whether birth date effects show up in their student populations. The methodology exists. Denmark has shown what works. Figure out when maturity levels even out and don't sort kids before that point. It might not be easy — cultural traditions, economic logistics, existing calendars all push back. But we have to ask ourselves: is short-term convenience worth writing off children before they've had a fair chance?

### Schools fight biology

Walker's *[Why We Sleep](/library/books/why-we-sleep/)* changed how I think about sleep entirely. It's not downtime — it's one of the most active biological processes for learning and brain development.

During adolescence the brain undergoes critical remodeling. Early childhood builds neural connections through REM sleep. Adolescence prunes them through deep NREM sleep — scaling back for efficiency. This pruning directly precedes improvements in cognitive skills, reasoning, and critical thinking. Deep sleep may be a driving force of brain maturation, not a byproduct.

Adolescents experience a biological shift in circadian rhythm. They
naturally fall asleep later and need to wake later — it's physiology,
not laziness. And then we force them to wake at 6 or 7 AM.

Memories formed without sleep are weaker, evaporating rapidly. The ability to regulate emotions depends on sufficient REM sleep night after night.

If you had to learn one thing about sleep: it's the thing keeping you alive just like air, but we all take it for granted. Believe me — as an adult I still struggle to maintain a regular sleep schedule. What chance does a teenager have when the system works against their biology?

Districts that have pushed school start times later see improvements in attendance, grades, and mental health. We know this works. We're just not doing it.

### Exams test endurance

Walker changed his own practice after realizing that end-loading exams forces students into sleep deprivation — directly opposing the goal of teaching. He split courses into thirds, made exams non-cumulative, and applied spaced learning. The faculty response was icy. They blamed the students.

I think policies should be informed by how memory actually works — spaced retrieval, sleep between study sessions, non-cumulative assessment. This doesn't require new technology. It requires accepting that the current format serves institutional convenience, not student learning.

### The education we never got

Our current system focuses so much on technical and career aspects — very little on emotions, kindness, or how to actually live with other people. De Botton puts it well: we're left to find our own path around our unfeasibly complicated minds, "a move as striking as suggesting that each generation should rediscover the laws of physics by themselves."

Goleman wrote in 1995 that each generation of children was more troubled emotionally than the last — more lonely, depressed, angry, impulsive. The trend has only accelerated — diagnosed mental health conditions among US adolescents rose 35 percent between 2016 and 2023.[^4] We should spend less time ranking children and more time helping them identify their natural competencies and gifts.

We're moving toward nuclear families and digital isolation. In India we used to have joint families where emotional knowledge was transmitted intergenerationally — imperfectly, but it was transmitted. That structure is disappearing. In some cultures it's still taboo to talk about emotions, particularly for men, who are often never taught a thing about their inner lives.

I think emotional literacy — self-awareness, empathy, conflict resolution, understanding attachment — should be part of school curricula from early on. Not as an elective but something we take seriously.

---

### What else we don't teach

In technical education we don't teach children to see how parts relate to wholes — to think in systems. Hamming argued that the ability to see the larger context of your work is what separates people who contribute from people who perform tasks.[^1] And we silo knowledge into departments when the most interesting work has always been interdisciplinary. Bell Labs did this well — their structure rewarded collaboration across disciplines by design.[^2] Our education system does the opposite.

But the gap I feel most personally is critical thinking — or more specifically, empirical skepticism. I've been watching Huberman and other podcasts for a while, and earlier whenever I'd see a fact on Telegram or Pinterest I'd just believe it. *Oh wow, what a cool thing.* Then I started consuming critics of these podcasts and they talk about the narratives and motives behind them. What hit me hardest was something I learned from Taleb's *Black Swan*[^5]: how easy it is to fool ourselves with narratives. We get swayed. It's very much possible that when a guest arrives on even a very credible podcast — even an expert in their field — they can make mistakes they don't know they're making. Do they read the methodology of the paper they're citing? Do they know if the results are generalizable — if the study was done on animals, or on a specific culture? They should at least mention it so the listener knows how things are being extrapolated. We live in a world where information overload is the problem. It's not about whether information exists — it's about what's actually true. And we don't teach people how to tell the difference.

---

The thing that frustrates me is that none of this is new. The research has been sitting in books for years, sometimes decades. The question we need to ask is: what's stopping us? Cultural tradition? Economic inertia? The fact that institutions change one generation at a time?

I think the uncomfortable answer is simpler. Our systems are built for adults, not children. School starts early because parents need to get to work. Exams are end-loaded because it's easier to administer. Cutoff dates are arbitrary because changing them is logistically inconvenient. Emotional education doesn't exist because nobody knows how to grade it. Every time there's a conflict between what's better for a child's development and what's more convenient for the adults running the system, convenience wins.

Maybe that's always been true. But the children growing up right now under arbitrary cutoff dates, sleep-deprived schedules, exam systems that punish their biology, and zero education about their own emotions — they don't have a generation to wait. It might not be easy to change things, but the fact that this research hasn't been converted to policy says a lot about who our systems are actually designed to serve.
